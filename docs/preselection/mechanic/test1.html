1rst Test

General Introduction​

This document presents a structured approach to 3D modeling and assembly using SOLIDWORKS®.
Throughout this guide, we will model four distinct parts and integrate them into an assembly alongside pre-supplied components.

By following this process, you will gain practical experience in:

  • Sketching and defining key features for each part.
  • Applying parametric design principles to ensure accuracy and flexibility.
  • Assembling components while managing constraints and interactions.

This step-by-step approach will help you understand essential design concepts and improve your proficiency in SOLIDWORKS.

What you need to know​

The SolidWorks software​

The SOLIDWORKS® CAD software is a parametric 3D mechanical design application that allows designers to quickly sketch ideas, experiment with features and dimensions, and produce accurate models and drawings.

This document explains the concepts and terminology of the SOLIDWORKS application and aims to familiarize you with the most commonly used features of SOLIDWORKS.

Target audience​

This document is a test report based on the modeling of certain parts and the creation of assemblies. Various design concepts and processes are explicitly presented to facilitate modeling for everyone.

The modeling of these parts serves as a practical training guide, leading you through the different stages of a process and presenting real-time results.

Design methods​

---> Planning the Model Creation Method

Before proceeding with the actual design of the model, it is useful to plan the creation method.
Once the requirements are identified and the appropriate concepts are developed, you can build the model:

----> Sketches Create sketches and decide on the dimensioning method and the locations for applying relations.

----> Features
Select the appropriate features, such as extrusions and fillets, determine the best features to apply, and the order of their application.

----> Assemblies Select the components to constrain and the types of constraints to apply.

--> Sketches​

The sketch serves as the foundation for most 3D models.
Model creation generally begins with a sketch. From the sketch, you can create features. You can combine one or more features to create a part. Then, you can combine and constrain the appropriate parts to create an assembly. From parts or assemblies, you can generate drawings.

A sketch is a 2D profile or cross-section. To create a 2D sketch, you use a plane or a flat face. In addition to 2D sketches, you can create 3D sketches that include a Z-axis along with the X and Y axes.

A sketch can be created in various ways. All sketches include the following elements:

--> Origin​

In many cases, you start the sketch from the origin, which provides an anchor point for the sketch.

--> Features​

Features are the fundamental operations that transform 2D or 3D sketches into volumes or complex shapes in SolidWorks.

----> Common Feature Types

  • Extrusion: Adds thickness to a sketch by extending it perpendicularly to its plane.
  • Revolution: Rotates a sketch around an axis to create revolved parts (e.g., shafts, pulleys).
  • Cut Extrusion: Removes material based on the sketch profile.
  • Fillet: Rounds edges to improve mechanical resistance or aesthetics.
  • Chamfer: Bevels an edge to facilitate assembly or eliminate sharp corners.
  • Shell: Hollows out a part to create a thin-walled structure.
  • Symmetry: Mirrors a portion of the part relative to a plane.

----> Application Method

  • Logical Order: It is important to plan the order of features. For example, it is better to extrude the main shape before adding details like holes or fillets.
  • Associativity: Any modification to a sketch or feature automatically affects subsequent features, making parametric design powerful and flexible.

--> Assemblies​

Assemblies allow multiple parts (part files) to be grouped in the same environment to simulate the final product.

----> Steps to Create an Assembly

  • Component Insertion: Import separately modeled parts into the assembly environment.
  • Assembly Constraints (Mates): Apply constraints to define the position and relative movement of parts. Examples:
    • Coincidence: Two faces or edges remain attached.
    • Parallelism/Perpendicularity: Maintains relative orientation.
    • Distance/Angle: Defines a fixed gap or angle between two entities.
    • Axis Alignment: Used for assembling cylindrical parts (shafts, holes).

----> Assembly Objectives

  • Fit Verification: Ensure all parts assemble correctly without interference.
  • Motion Simulation: Test relative movements (e.g., rotation, translation) to validate mechanical functionality.
  • Collision Analysis: Detect potential interferences or unwanted contacts between parts.
  • Drawing Preparation: Generate exploded views and bill of materials for manufacturing or assembly.

Assembly test​

This section of the test consists of assembling different parts to obtain a mechanical gripper. After downloading the ZIP file, we proceed with assembling the provided parts while ensuring that the necessary constraints are applied.

Clarification of Concepts​

Assembly in SolidWorks is an essential step in 3D modeling, allowing you to combine multiple individual parts (called components) to form a complete and functional mechanical system. With this feature, you can:

  • Simulate the real behavior of a mechanism,
  • Check movements and interferences,
  • Analyze physical properties like mass and center of gravity.

Constraints and Features Used for the Gripper​

🔩 "Coaxial" Constraint​

The coaxial constraint aligns two cylindrical or circular axes (holes, shafts, cylinders) so that they share the same central axis.

Example: aligning a shaft with its corresponding bore.

📏 "Coincident" Constraint​

The coincident constraint forces two flat or linear faces to touch — that is, to be coplanar or merged.

Purpose: to attach one part against another.

🔁 "Symmetric Components" Feature​

Part of the “Linear Component Pattern” section.
It allows you to create a symmetrical copy of a parent component with respect to a reference plane.

Assembling the Parts​

Steps:​

  1. Open SolidWorks
  2. Open the ASSEMBLAGE PINCE file
  3. Complete the missing parts

Parts to Insert:​

  • Connecting rods
  • Jaw holder
  • Left jaw
  • Right jaw
  • Connecting rod pin
  • Jaw holder pin
  • CHC screw M5×16
  • CHC screw M5×25
  • Circlips

Assembly Procedure:​

  1. Insert the first connecting rod:

    • Use coaxial and coincident constraints
    • Position it above the actuator rod end.

  2. Insert the second connecting rod:

    • Placed underneath the first, with the actuator rod end between them.
    • Add a coincident constraint between the two connecting rod faces for perfect alignment.

  3. Use the "Symmetric Components" command (with the top plane) to obtain 4 connecting rods.

  4. Insert the "jaw holder" part using coaxial and coincident constraints, then mirror it as before.

  5. Insert the jaws (left and right) and the fastening components:

    • CHC screw M5×16
    • CHC screw M5×25
    • Connecting rod pin
    • Jaw holder pin
    • Circlips

🔧 Result: A complete mechanical gripper.
